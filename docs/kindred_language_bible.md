# Kindred Language Bible

## 1. The Job of the Language

The language does not sell.

The language does not persuade.

The language does one thing:

**It notices something true… and lets the reader recognize themselves.**

If they feel seen → they lean in  
If they feel sold → they pull away

---

## 2. The Core Tone (Non-Negotiable)

Kindred speaks like this:

- Quiet
- Observant
- Certain
- Slightly detached (in a good way)

It never sounds like:

- A brand
- A teacher
- A system
- A “solution”

---

## 3. The Prime Directive

Every line must feel like:

> “I’ve thought this before.”

Not:

> “This is a good idea.”

---

## 4. Sentence Rules (Hard Constraints)

### Rule 1: Short beats long
- Most sentences under 12 words
- Occasionally longer for rhythm, not explanation

### Rule 2: One idea per sentence

Bad:

> People drift because life gets busy and we forget to reach out.

Good:

> People drift.  
> Life gets busy.

### Rule 3: No explaining

Bad:

> This happens because memory isn’t reliable.

Good:

> Sometimes, it’s just quietly gone.

### Rule 4: No instructions

Bad:

> You should reach out.

Good:

> You think, I should reach out.

### Rule 5: No hype words

Avoid:

- powerful
- simple
- easy
- best
- revolutionary

---

## 5. Structural Pattern

Most writing follows this flow:

### 1. Observation

> There are people I think about from time to time.

### 2. Expansion

> I’ll be walking, or driving…

### 3. Turn

> But I don’t.

### 4. Quiet Truth

> Thinking about someone is not the same as showing up.

---

## 6. Language Patterns to Reuse

### A. The Almost Action

- I should…
- I meant to…
- I’ll remember that.

### B. The Drift

- It just… fades.
- The moment passes.
- The day fills in around it.

### C. The Quiet Contradiction

> It feels like enough.  
> But it isn’t.

### D. The Object Lesson

Use physical things to express abstract ideas:

- a book
- a game
- a song
- a domain

### E. The Unresolved Ending

Do not tie everything up. Leave space.

---

## 9. Rhythm Rules

### Use white space

Let lines breathe.

### Use fragments

> But I don’t.  
> He paused.  
> It just waits.

### Use repetition sparingly

> They know.  
> They remember.  
> They notice.

---

## 10. What This Writing Is Really About

This is not about:

- books
- people
- memory
- objects

This is about:

**attention**

- what we notice
- what we hold
- what we let drift

---

## 11. Connection to Kindred

The writing prepares the reader for the product.

Without explaining the product.

Example:

> Thinking about someone is not the same as showing up.

Kindred exists as the place where they show up.

---

## 12. Editing Checklist

Before publishing:

1. Did I explain anything? → cut it
2. Did I tell them what to do? → remove it
3. Does this sound like advice? → rewrite it
4. Does this feel slightly incomplete? → good
5. Can someone see themselves in this? → required

---

## 13. Do / Don’t Examples (From Small Things)

### Example 1 — Memory

**DO:**

> Sometimes, it’s just quietly gone.

**DON’T:**

> Memory isn’t built to hold onto everything that matters.

Why: The “don’t” explains. The “do” lets the reader feel it.

---

### Example 2 — Showing Up

**DO:**

> Thinking about someone is not the same as showing up.

**DON’T:**

> You need to reach out to people more often.

Why: The “don’t” instructs. The “do” reveals a truth.

---

### Example 3 — Objects / Middle Space

**DO:**

> It just… waits.

**DON’T:**

> These things exist in a kind of unresolved middle space.

Why: The “don’t” abstracts. The “do” is visible and felt.

---

### Example 4 — Drift

**DO:**

> The moment passes.

**DON’T:**

> Life gets busy and prevents you from acting.

Why: The “don’t” analyzes. The “do” observes.

---

### Example 5 — Internal Dialogue

**DO:**

> I’ll remember that.

**DON’T:**

> I trust my memory to retain important ideas.

Why: The “don’t” sounds intellectual. The “do” sounds human.

---

## 14. Final Principle

Kindred does not argue.

Kindred does not convince.

Kindred recognizes something true.

And lets the reader nod.
